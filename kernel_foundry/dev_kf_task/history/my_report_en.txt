Kernel Foundry GEMM Optimization Work Report (Expanded)
Date: 2026-05-25

## 1. Project Objective

This project evaluates LLM-driven evolutionary optimization for FP16 GEMM on Intel Battlemage B580.

- Hardware: Intel Battlemage G21 (B580), 20 Xe2 cores
- FP16 XMX peak: 96 TFLOPS
- Memory bandwidth: ~456 GB/s
- L2 cache: ~24 MB

Target kernel:
- C[2048,2560] = A[2048,2048] × B[2048,2560]
- Compute workload: 21.5 GFLOP
- Theoretical lower bound: ~0.224 ms (compute-bound, high OI)

Key primitive:
- intel_sub_group_f16_f16_matrix_mad_k16(...)
- 4096 FLOPs per invocation (8×16×16 FP16 MAD)

Baseline performance:
- Runtime: 33.9 ms
- Throughput: 0.63 TFLOPS
- XMX utilization: 0.7%
Project Objective
## 2. Method and Experimental Design

Optimization was performed through iterative evolutionary search (v4–v11), varying:

1) LLM sampling temperature (0.0 to 0.3)
2) Seed kernel strategy (no seed, warm start from previous best)
3) Prompt guidance strength (generic to explicit bottleneck instructions)
4) Multi-branch generation and hardware-validated selection

Core idea:
- LLM proposes mutated kernels
- Framework validates correctness + benchmark speed
- Strong candidates become seeds for next rounds

## 6. Technical Findings

1) Temperature is the primary evolution control variable.
- 0.0: nearly no useful architecture mutation
- 0.1–0.2: incremental progress only
- 0.25: best balance between creativity and stability (breakthrough regime)
- 0.3: higher regression risk near optimum

2) Seed quality determines whether budget goes to “correctness” or “optimization”.
- No seed: many trials spent on reaching workable structure
- v4 seed: unlocked rapid optimization (v10 breakthrough)
- v10 seed + high temp: tends to damage already-good structure

3) Architectural mutation can outperform textbook intuition.
- Breakthrough came from a non-obvious architecture shift rather than pure micro-tuning.

4) On this GPU, occupancy and latency hiding dominated algorithm-level tricks.
- Some theoretically “better” ideas (e.g., heavier buffering / larger resource footprint) reduced practical performance.

5) Model consistency matters.
- Mixed-model evolution (v5) showed poor inheritance continuity.
- Strong single-model runs (opus-only) produced most of the gains.

## 7. Current Limits

- Strong dependence on high-quality model reasoning
- Utilization plateau around ~21% XMX
- High end-to-end evaluation cost (compile + verify + benchmark + inference)
- No adaptive temperature control implemented yet
- Limited compiler-awareness feedback (IGC-level effects not closed-loop)

## 8. Recommended Next Steps

Priority A — Short term (highest impact):
1) Fine-tune around v10 with lower temperature (0.15–0.2)
2) Constrain mutations to local improvements (avoid architecture-destroying jumps)

Priority B — Medium term:
3) Add compiler-guided loop: collect IGC ISA dumps and feed salient signals back to the model
4) Introduce adaptive temperature scheduling (explore → exploit → reheat on stagnation)

Priority C — Search quality:
5) Refine behavior descriptors / archive granularity to preserve high-potential variants
6) Use staged evolution: architecture discovery first, micro-optimization second

## 9. Final Conclusion

This project demonstrates that evolutionary LLM-driven kernel optimization can produce major, hardware-validated gains on Intel GPU GEMM workloads. The v10 result (1.07ms, 31.7x speedup, 20.9% XMX utilization, 100% correctness) confirms the method’s practical value. Future improvements are likely to come from tighter compiler-aware feedback and adaptive search control rather than brute-force evaluation scaling.
